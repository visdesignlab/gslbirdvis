Finding the Flight of
Least Resistance
by: Zoe Exelbert

The Great Salt Lake is a prominent stopping point for over 10 million birds year-round. Situated on the Pacific Flyway Route, the Great Salt Lake is a top attraction to over 338 species who utilize the lake's resources for breeding, feeding, and temporary habitats during seasonal migration periods.

The flight to the Great Salt Lake for these birds has never been questioned, as it is an evolved response to the Great Salt Lake's continuous offering of food and habitat safety. However, as the climate begins to warm, water levels are dropping and food is becoming more scarce.

Two species in specific, the Eared Grebe and the American White Pelican, are extremely vulnerable to the environmental deterioration of the lake. With the majority of their continental population coming to the GSL every year, if conditions continue to worsen, both of these species are in danger.

Here we will investigate the climate effects these birds are vulnerable to as we fly along with them over the years, answering the question, is flying to the Great Salt lake still the flight of least resistance?

The Eared Grebe, the most abundant grebe species in the world, begins their flight South from the Pacific Northwest towards the Great Salt Lake around September.

During this time in the Fall, Grebes come to stock up on food before heading to Mexico for the winter. Then, in the Spring, the Grebes will come back to breed and nest at the GSL.

Over an entire year, the Great Salt Lake hosts anywhere from 2 to 5 million Eared Grebes, which at times is almost the entire North American population. As the Grebes travel between Mexico and the Great Salt Lake, the associated climate with these locations largely directs the success of their migration. When the Grebes are wintering in Mexico before making their journey to the GSL, they are heavily dependent on the fish supply available. To complete this long, non-stop journey, they must consume substantial amounts of fish beforehand. However, fish populations in these Mexican waters can experience significant stress due to climate events, which may impact the Grebes' ability to adequately prepare for their migration. During an El NiÃ±o event, the warming of ocean waters negatively impacts fish populations. Fish typically thrive in cooler waters, so when ocean temperatures rise, they are either compelled to migrate to cooler areas or may not survive at all. In response to these sudden adaptations, fish often retreat to deeper, cooler waters where they can more easily thrive. However, this presents a challenge for Grebes, as their small stature limits their diving depth. With fish swimming beyond their maximum foraging depth, Grebes must expend significant energy attempting deeper dives or searching for new feeding grounds where fish remain within reach. The complete opposite occurs during a La NiÃ±a. Colder waters during these times allow for fish to thrive extremely well, now able to swim at their usual depth, making for some very happy Grebes. Changes in fish populations during climate events directly affect the Grebes, as their survival and migration depend on a steady fish supply. When fish numbers drop during an El NiÃ±o, Grebes are forced to adapt to these conditions. These climate shifts may cause Grebes to delay their migration to the Great Salt Lake in the Spring as they try to follow the fish to their new locations in search of food. However, if they are still not adequately fed, their lack of energy might prevent them from even attempting the journey. The Great Salt Lake has also undergone significant climate changes, making matters even worse for the Grebes. Not only are they faced with challenges during their wintering period in Mexico, but their time at the lake is becoming more stressful.

The Great Salt Lake has undergone significant landscape and ecological transformations due to rising local temperatures driven by climate change. Illustrated in this graph, the lake has dropped significantly in the past 20 years. To restore the lake to a healthy level, it will take many years of consistent, heavy winters and rainfall to accomplish this. Even with optimal weather patterns, refilling the lake will be slow and uncertain, as the amount of water needed to return it to its previous size and ecological health is immense. The Great Salt Lake has been a historical attraction to birds solely because of the availability of brine shrimp. Eared Grebes are extremely reliant on a healthy brine shrimp supply, needing to consume 28,000 adult brine shrimp daily to survive. This equates to approximately 7-8 hours of foraging a day, which means Grebes need to consume 1 brine shrimp per second in order to meet their dietary needs. As the water levels at the Great Salt Lake decline, the resulting increase in salinity places significant stress on the brine shrimp populations, which can lead to their decline. Brine shrimp can only survive within a specific salt concentration range, and with this ongoing trend of decreasing lake levels, the rising salinity levels present a serious threat to their survival. Just like the fish supply in Mexico, the survival of brine shrimp at the Great Salt Lake is closely linked to the well-being of the Grebes. With such a strict dietary plan, any disruption in the availability of these shrimp puts the Grebes in a highly vulnerable position. On top of this, Grebes tend to either stay at the lake through the summer or return in September from a more Northern location they traveled to. Starting around October, Grebes undergo molting, a process in which they shed and regrow feathers. The molting process allows Grebes to grow a new set of feathers and gain weight in preparation for their fall migration back to Mexico. Because Grebes are flightless during this time, known as the fall-staging period, these birds are entirely dependent on the lake's local food resources. If their immediate environment fails to provide adequate sustenance, they have no alternative food sources and face severe survival challenges. When the lakeâ€™s menu is slim, a feather might be the best on the menu. Grebes go through a very similar cycle to the fall-staging period 3-6 times each year, during which they become flightless to conserve energy resources. This renders the Grebes flightless for almost 9-10 months each year, the longest flightless period of any bird in the world capable of flight. With the Great Salt Lake historically being host to nearly the entire national population of Eared Grebes, these risks are large. Bound to long periods of flightlessness and ongoing food challenges both in Mexico and at the Great Salt Lake, Grebes are facing real threats to their population survival.

The Great Salt Lake has experienced dramatic changes in landscape and ecological health as local temperatures have risen as a result of climate change.

Northern Utah temperatures have increased almost 2 degrees Farenheit since the early 1980s, which has evidently caused declines at the Great Salt Lake. On top of this, inflows from the Bear, Weber, and Jordan rivers have gradually declined over the past 40 years. Not only do these rivers provide water to the lake, but they support the diets of many Pelicans. The Great Salt Lake has been a historical attraction to birds solely because of the availability of brine shrimp. Yet, Pelicans do not typically consume the brine shrimp supply available at the lake. Their diet mainly consists of fish, which cannot be found in the lake given it's high salty content. These fish are available at freshwater locations, the closest ones being the Bear, Weber, and Jordan rivers. If these local rivers do not have sufficient food supply, as a result of low water levels, Pelicans will travel anywhere from 40 to 200 miles to get their fish fix. As you can see in this graph, the lake has dropped over 5 feet in the past 20 years. While this may not seem like a lot, it will take many years of consistent, heavy winters and rainfall to restore the lake to a healthy, sustainable level. Even with ideal weather patterns, the process of refilling the lake will be slow and uncertain, as the amount of water needed to bring it back to its former size and ecological health is immense. Gunnison Island, the Pelicans main hub at the lake, is one of the most visibly affected areas by the declining water levels. The island is no longer a true island or isolated habitat, and is now better denoted as a "land bridge". The shrinking of the lake has connected what was once Gunnison Island with the perimeter of the lake, allowing coyotes and other predators easy access by crossing these land bridges.
